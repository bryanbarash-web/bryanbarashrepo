Methodology
How these numbers were derived

Every market denominator in this analysis is a blended estimate constructed from up to four independent data sources: official state agency publications, CRB Monitor's active retail license database (as of February 28, 2026), MJ BIZ Factbook 2025 projections, and Emerald Intelligence dispensary address counts (March 2026). Rather than selecting a single source per state, we apply a three-factor weighting model to each data point before blending. Factor one is a base source weight reflecting data quality — official state data receives 90, CRB Monitor 85 (reduced to 70 where its count exceeds official figures by more than 20%, indicating inclusion of non-operational licenses), MJ BIZ 75, and Emerald 70 (boosted to 85 in states where Emerald methodology has been independently validated). Factor two is a time-decay adjustment that reduces each source's effective weight by 5 points per month of distance from the March 11, 2026 reference date in stable markets, and 10 points per month in high-velocity states (New York, Ohio, Minnesota, New Jersey, Maryland, Arizona). Critically, CRB Monitor (Feb 28, 2026) and Emerald Intelligence (March 2026) are treated as essentially current — at +0.4 and 0.0 months from reference respectively — and carry near-full base weight with minimal decay. The sources requiring meaningful forward projection are official state revenue publications (2024 annuals centered June 2024 = +21.3 months; 2025 annuals centered June 2025 = +9.3 months) and MJ BIZ Factbook estimates (centered June 2025 = +9.3 months). Factor three is a velocity multiplier — each data point is projected to the March 11, 2026 reference date so all sources are compared on an apples-to-apples basis regardless of when they were published. Critically, this projection uses piecewise S-curve growth rates rather than flat monthly rates: for states with sufficient public data (New York, Ohio, Colorado, Michigan, Washington, California, Oregon, Minnesota, New Jersey, Maryland), we fit segmented rates across distinct market windows using actual published monthly sales figures, so that a flat +6%/month rate is not incorrectly compounded over 14 months when the true trajectory shows clear deceleration. For other states, a tapered rate is applied — the distant-period rate is scaled up to 1.4× the current rate, reflecting that older data points occurred during a faster-moving phase of the market cycle. The blended denominator is the weighted average of velocity-adjusted source values, with each source's time-decayed weight as its coefficient.

Two additional refinements were applied to improve structural accuracy. First, rooftop and GMV denominators are built from separate source pools: the rooftop denominator draws exclusively from license databases (CRB Monitor, Emerald Intelligence, official state licensee counts), while the GMV denominator draws exclusively from revenue publications (official state sales data, MJ BIZ Factbook projections). This separation eliminates cross-contamination between license counts and revenue figures — phenomena that move at fundamentally different speeds and diverge sharply in markets like Michigan (where store count still grows while revenue contracts) or Maine (where dual-license complexity inflates the store denominator without affecting sales). Second, a directional disagreement multiplier is applied after initial blending: in growth markets, sources reading higher than the inter-source mean receive a weight bonus (up to 1.25×) because leading indicators tend to be more current in expanding markets, while license databases in declining markets receive a penalty when they read above the mean (as inactive licenses persist after closures). In stable markets, official state data receives a modest 1.1× preference over third-party license counts. Confidence intervals reflect source count, coefficient of variation across velocity-adjusted values, market phase, and known structural anomalies. GMV shares above 100% are not errors — they reflect Dutchie's 30-day annualized snapshot capturing an atypical period relative to the state denominator, and are flagged explicitly. For 13 states where Headset publishes public market data (AZ, CA, CO, IL, MA, MD, MI, MO, NV, NY, OH, OR, WA), Headset's February 2026 annualized monthly sales figure is included as an additional GMV source at weight 80. Headset data is sourced from 3,500+ POS-connected retailers and is treated as current (February 28, 2026 ≈ +0.4 months from reference). This is particularly impactful for high-velocity or declining states where annual figures diverge from recent run rates — Ohio's blended GMV denominator resolves from $2.86B to $1.77B once the current Headset monthly is included, producing a more credible 79.8% GMV share. States with Headset data are marked HS in the Sources column. Where ground-truth official data is definitive and current, it overrides the blending model entirely: Alabama (3 licensed sites, no sales commenced as of March 2026) and Minnesota (OCM official counts as of March 2, 2026) use official figures directly. All GMV values are annualized millions of dollars. External citation should use only HIGH and MEDIUM confidence states.
